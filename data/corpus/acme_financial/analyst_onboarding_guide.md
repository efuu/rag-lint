# Analyst Onboarding — First 60 Days

Welcome to the firm. This guide is written by recent analyst classes for incoming ones. It is meant to save you from the small frictions that no one tells you about in the formal orientation and to point you at the people and resources you will actually rely on.

## Your first week

Most of your first week is taken up by IT provisioning, compliance training, and meeting your deal team. None of it is glamorous. Use the downtime between sessions to read the three most recent committee memos in your sector — those are the clearest picture of how the senior partners think.

Your laptop comes preloaded with the firm's approved software list. If you think you need something that is not on it, ask IT before trying to install anything yourself; we are a regulated shop and sideloading is taken seriously.

## How to ask for help

The single highest-leverage habit you can build in your first month is learning who to ask what. A quick pattern: for a modeling or valuation question, ask a second-year on your deal team. For a process question, ask the deal-team associate. For a strategic framing question, bring it to the VP in your one-on-one. Do not ask the partners structural questions that someone more junior can answer faster.

## The first deal you stay on

You will rotate through two or three live situations in your first sixty days. One of them will probably become "your" deal in the sense that you will staff it to closing or to a no-go decision. The most useful thing to do on that deal is to keep a running list of the questions the committee asks that you cannot answer on the spot; those are the gaps in your mental model and they will show up again.

## Committee memos

Our memos are shorter than the industry average. That is by design: we believe that a memo that cannot be summarized in a page of prose is a memo whose thesis is not yet clear. Do not pad; do not hedge. Say what you think, why you think it, and what would change your mind.

## Staying healthy

The job will compress in waves. Protect your health during the low-intensity weeks so that you can absorb the high-intensity ones. This is not optional, and it is not a sign of weakness.
