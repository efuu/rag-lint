# The State of the Middle-Market Private Credit Opportunity

A sector note for limited partners and the broader investor community. This piece reflects our on-the-ground view of deal flow in the middle market as of the fourth quarter of 2025 and does not represent any specific fund's positioning.

## Where we see spreads today

Originations on senior secured unitranche loans to sponsor-backed middle-market borrowers cleared at an average of S+525 basis points during the third quarter, compared with S+565 basis points at this point a year ago. The tightening reflects an increased number of lenders chasing a still-constrained pipeline, not a change in underlying credit quality.

Second-lien and mezzanine tranches have compressed less. We attribute the slower compression to the thinner bench of specialist lenders who will underwrite the junior capital stack, particularly in cyclical sectors.

## Deal flow

Pipeline through our conversations with the twenty-eight mid-market sponsors we track most closely has remained stable quarter on quarter, with a modest uptick in sponsors exploring continuation vehicles as an alternative to a conventional exit. These vehicles have become a meaningful source of first-lien financing opportunities where the sponsor is rolling their own equity alongside new LP capital.

Among the sponsors we spoke with in the fourth quarter, Meridian Capital cited continuation-vehicle dynamics as a specific driver of their 2026 pipeline planning.

## What could change this

Three things would shift our read of the opportunity. A sustained retreat in base rates would compress absolute yields faster than spreads would widen to compensate. A meaningful pickup in syndicated-loan issuance would redirect borrowers back toward public markets at the larger end of the middle market. And a default wave in the lower middle market would expose the thinner workout benches among the newer direct-lending entrants.

## Closing thought

We remain constructive on the asset class over a full cycle but skeptical of any simple extrapolation from the last three years. The borrowers who generated the vintage-return benchmarks of 2020 through 2022 borrowed into a very different macro, and the mix of lenders competing for the next cohort is not the same as the one that underwrote those loans.
